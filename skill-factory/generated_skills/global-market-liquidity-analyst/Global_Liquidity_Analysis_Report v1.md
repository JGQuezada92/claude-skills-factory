# **1\. GLOBAL LIQUIDITY CYCLE ASSESSMENT**

## **1.1 Current Cycle Positioning**

The global liquidity cycle, as pioneered by Michael Howell of CrossBorder Capital, represents the ebb and flow of money through the international financial system. This cycle typically follows a 60-65 month pattern, driven primarily by coordinated central bank balance sheet expansions and contractions, rather than traditional interest rate policy alone.

Think of global liquidity like ocean tides—*just as tides are driven by gravitational forces beyond any single point on the coast, global liquidity is driven by the collective 'gravitational pull' of major central banks expanding or contracting their balance sheets*. When central banks simultaneously expand (as in 2020-2022), it creates a 'high tide' that lifts all asset boats. When they contract (as in 2022-2024), it creates a 'low tide' that exposes vulnerabilities.

**KEY INSIGHT: Current Cycle Phase**

The current liquidity cycle began in **September 2022** when global central bank assets bottomed following the most aggressive monetary tightening campaign in decades. As of November 2025, the cycle is approximately **38 months old**, placing it at roughly **58% completion** based on the historical 60-65 month average.

This positioning is critical: we are in the **late expansion phase**, approaching the cyclical peak. Historically, this phase is characterized by:

1. Decelerating liquidity growth rates (currently \-3.9% YoY for G4 CB assets, improving from \-7% earlier in 2025\)  
2. Rotation from early-cycle winners (technology, growth stocks) to late-cycle beneficiaries (commodities, financials, value)  
3. Dollar weakening as relative monetary policy diverges (US slowing QT while maintaining rates)  
4. Increased market volatility as investors anticipate the turning point  
5. Rising commodity prices, particularly gold, as inflation hedge demand increases

## **1.2 Historical Context: Learning from Past Cycles**

To understand where we are, we must examine where we've been. The global financial system has experienced four major liquidity cycles since the 2008 Global Financial Crisis:

**Cycle 1 (2009-2014): 63 months**  
The post-GFC recovery cycle was characterized by unprecedented quantitative easing programs (QE1, QE2, QE3). Central bank assets expanded from $5T to $15T globally. This cycle peaked in Q2 2014 as the Federal Reserve began tapering asset purchases. The subsequent taper tantrum demonstrated markets' sensitivity to liquidity withdrawal, with the S\&P 500 declining 8% and emerging market currencies cratering.

**Cycle 2 (2014-2019): 61 months**  
This cycle saw the Federal Reserve attempt policy normalization through quantitative tightening (2017-2019), while the ECB and BOJ maintained ultra-accommodative policies. China provided significant stimulus in 2015-2016 to counter the emerging market crisis. The cycle peaked in Q4 2019 with the repo market crisis, forcing the Fed to reverse course and inject emergency liquidity.

**Cycle 3 (2019-2022): 34 months (shortened)**  
The COVID-19 pandemic created an emergency liquidity cycle. Central banks injected over $10T in 18 months—the most aggressive expansion in history. This cycle was artificially shortened as inflation surged to 9% in the US and 11% in Europe, forcing premature tightening. The cycle peaked in Q1 2022 and reversed violently, with both stocks and bonds experiencing historic simultaneous declines.

**Cycle 4 (2022-Present): 38 months and counting**  
The current cycle began as central banks drained nearly $3T in liquidity through aggressive QT. The cycle bottomed in September 2022 and has been slowly expanding since, though at rates well below historical norms. The expansion has been driven more by the *slowdown of QT* than by outright balance sheet expansion—a subtle but crucial distinction.

# **2\. CENTRAL BANK BALANCE SHEET ANALYSIS**

Central bank balance sheets are the **primary driver** of global liquidity. The four major central banks—the Federal Reserve, European Central Bank, Bank of Japan, and People's Bank of China—collectively control approximately $26 trillion in assets, representing roughly 27% of global M2 money supply.

## **2.1 Federal Reserve: QT Deceleration Provides Critical Support**

**Current Balance Sheet: $6.6 trillion**  
**Peak (April 2022): $8.9 trillion**  
**Cumulative Reduction: \-$2.3 trillion (-26%)**

The Federal Reserve has executed the largest quantitative tightening program in history, draining $2.3 trillion from the financial system since June 2022\. However, *the pace of QT has dramatically slowed*:

* Initial pace (June 2022-May 2024): $95 billion per month ($60B Treasuries \+ $35B MBS)  
* First slowdown (June 2024-March 2025): $60 billion per month ($25B Treasuries \+ $35B MBS)  
* Current pace (April 2025-present): $40 billion per month ($5B Treasuries \+ $35B MBS)

This **80% reduction** in Treasury runoff is economically significant. To understand why, consider that the Federal Reserve is not just shrinking its balance sheet—it's removing a major source of demand from the Treasury market. At the initial pace, the Fed was essentially forcing the private sector to absorb an additional $60 billion of Treasuries monthly. At the current pace, this absorption requirement has dropped to $5 billion monthly.

Think of this like a bathtub analogy: The government is running water into the tub through deficit spending (adding about $150B per month in new Treasury issuance). The Fed's QT is like opening the drain, letting water out. By dramatically closing that drain (from $95B to $40B monthly), the water level (liquidity) drains more slowly, supporting asset prices.

**FORWARD IMPACT: Fed Balance Sheet**

The Fed's *QT slowdown is not altruistic*—it reflects approaching the estimated level of 'ample reserves' ($3.0-3.5T). Once reserves reach this threshold, the Fed must halt QT to prevent money market dysfunction. We estimate this occurs in **Q3-Q4 2026**, at which point the balance sheet will stabilize around $6.0-6.2 trillion. This stabilization will provide a floor under liquidity but not meaningfully expand it, setting up neutral-to-negative conditions for 2027\.

## **2.2 European Central Bank: Gradual Normalization Continues**

**Current Balance Sheet: €6.2 trillion ($6.7T USD equivalent)**  
**Peak (2022): €8.9 trillion**  
**Cumulative Reduction: \-€2.7 trillion (-30%)**

The ECB has been unwinding its pandemic emergency purchase programme (PEPP) since July 2023, with no reinvestment of maturing securities. Additionally, the asset purchase programme (APP) holdings have declined by €353 billion. Unlike the Fed's explicit caps, the ECB allows its holdings to decline at the pace of natural maturities—currently running at approximately €40-50 billion per month combined.

The ECB faces a unique challenge: **fragmentation risk**. As liquidity drains, peripheral European sovereigns (Italy, Spain, Portugal) face higher funding costs relative to Germany. The ECB has maintained its Transmission Protection Instrument (TPI) as a backstop, but has not yet needed to activate it. However, Italian 10-year yields trading 140bps above German bunds signal building stress.

**FORWARD IMPACT: ECB Balance Sheet**

The ECB will likely be forced to slow its balance sheet runoff in **H1 2026** as peripheral spreads widen beyond political tolerance levels. We project the ECB balance sheet stabilizes around €5.5-5.8 trillion, implying another €400-700 billion decline. This continued liquidity drain from Europe partially offsets US and China easing, maintaining the 'mixed picture' in global liquidity.

## **2.3 Bank of Japan: Historic Pivot from Extreme Accommodation**

**Current Balance Sheet: ¥695 trillion ($4.6T USD equivalent)**  
**Peak (early 2024): ¥750 trillion**  
**Cumulative Reduction: \-¥55 trillion (-7%)**

The Bank of Japan's policy evolution represents the most significant regime shift in global monetary policy. After maintaining yield curve control (YCC) and negative interest rates for nearly a decade, the BOJ ended negative rates in March 2024 and has begun allowing its massive JGB holdings to run off naturally.

The BOJ faces a delicate balancing act. Japan's debt-to-GDP ratio exceeds 260%—the highest in the developed world. The BOJ owns approximately 50% of the outstanding JGB market. As it steps back from purchases, private sector investors must absorb significantly higher volumes. This has already caused 10-year JGB yields to rise from 0% to 1.2%, the highest since 2008\.

To understand the global impact, consider the *carry trade unwind mechanism*: For years, global investors borrowed yen at negative rates to invest in higher-yielding assets worldwide. As Japanese rates rise, this trade unwinds, forcing asset sales globally. The August 2024 'carry trade unwind' that triggered a global equity sell-off was just the beginning—we expect periodic volatility bursts as positions continue unwinding through 2025-2026.

**FORWARD IMPACT: BOJ Balance Sheet**

The BOJ's balance sheet will likely decline another **¥50-80 trillion** through 2026, reaching ¥615-645 trillion. This represents a **significant liquidity headwind** for global markets. However, the pace will be gradual to avoid destabilizing Japanese government financing. The risk factor is political pressure: if JGB yields approach 2%, the government may force the BOJ to halt QT, creating a 'fiscal dominance' scenario that would be yen-negative and inflation-positive.

## **2.4 People's Bank of China: Targeted Stimulus Without Flood**

**Current Balance Sheet: ¥47.1 trillion CNY ($6.6T USD equivalent)**  
**Growth Rate: \+1.8% YoY**

China's liquidity management differs fundamentally from Western central banks. Rather than balance sheet size, the PBOC relies on reserve requirement ratios (RRR), lending facilities (MLF, SLF), and targeted liquidity injections. The balance sheet has grown modestly but steadily, reflecting currency issuance and foreign exchange purchases rather than quantitative easing.

The critical question: ***Will China 'open the floodgates'*** with massive stimulus? Recent actions suggest targeted rather than broad-based easing:

* RRR cuts totaling 50bps in 2024-2025, releasing \~¥1 trillion in liquidity  
* MLF operations increased to ¥7.5 trillion outstanding  
* 'Swap facilities' for securities firms announced but not yet fully implemented  
* Local government debt support programs (¥3 trillion for refinancing)

However, *credit demand remains weak*, not supply. Chinese M2 growth of only 1.5% YoY despite easing measures indicates businesses and consumers are not borrowing even when liquidity is available. This is reminiscent of Japan's 'liquidity trap' in the 1990s—you can lead a horse to water, but you can't make it drink.

**FORWARD IMPACT: PBOC Balance Sheet**

China is likely to announce a **fiscal stimulus package** in the ¥3-5 trillion CNY range through 2025-2026, partially funded through PBOC balance sheet expansion. This would add approximately **$200-350 billion** to global liquidity annually. While significant in absolute terms, it's modest relative to GDP and unlikely to replicate the impact of past Chinese stimulus cycles (2009, 2016). The key beneficiaries would be commodities (particularly industrial metals) and emerging market equities with China exposure.

# **3\. MONETARY AGGREGATES REVIEW**

While central bank balance sheets show the *supply* of liquidity, monetary aggregates (M2, M3) show how that liquidity translates into *usable money* in the economy. The divergence between these measures reveals critical insights about liquidity efficiency and velocity.

## **3.1 Global M2: The Liquidity Paradox**

**Current Global M2: \~$96.0 trillion**  
**YoY Growth: \+1.1%**  
**Historical Average: 4-6%**

Here's the paradox: Central banks have injected trillions in liquidity since 2020, yet M2 growth—the broadest measure of money available to spend—is growing at only 1.1% globally. This is **well below the rate needed to sustain nominal GDP growth**, which typically requires M2 growth of 4-6% annually (real GDP growth \+ inflation target).

Where did the liquidity go? It's *trapped in the financial system*. Consider these data points:

* US banks hold $3.3 trillion in reserves at the Federal Reserve (compared to $1.5 trillion pre-pandemic)  
* US Treasury General Account (TGA) at the Fed: $750 billion (historically \~$300 billion)  
* Reverse Repo facility usage has declined from $2.5T peak but remains elevated at $300 billion  
* Money market fund assets exceed $6.5 trillion (vs. $4T pre-pandemic)

This represents approximately **$4-5 trillion of 'dormant' liquidity**—money that exists on balance sheets but isn't circulating through the economy. Think of it like a traffic jam: all the cars (money) are present, but they're not moving (not creating economic velocity).

# **4\. CROSS-BORDER CAPITAL FLOWS**

Cross-border capital flows represent the international transmission mechanism of liquidity. Money doesn't just stay in the country where it's created—it flows globally seeking the best risk-adjusted returns. Understanding these flows is crucial for positioning in currencies, emerging markets, and commodities.

The US dollar's role as the global reserve currency creates a unique dynamic. Approximately 60% of global trade is invoiced in dollars, and 60% of central bank reserves are held in dollar-denominated assets. When the Federal Reserve expands its balance sheet, it creates dollars that flow worldwide. When it contracts (QT), it creates a global dollar shortage.

# **5\. DEBT-LIQUIDITY INTERDEPENDENCE**

## **5.1 The $7 Trillion Maturity Wall: The Single Biggest Risk**

Between 2026 and 2027, approximately **$7 trillion in global debt matures annually**, requiring refinancing at substantially higher interest rates than when originally issued. This represents 7-8% of global GDP and creates a structural drain on liquidity that will dominate the 2026-2027 period.

To understand the magnitude, consider a simple example: A corporation that issued $1 billion in bonds in 2021 at 2% interest now faces refinancing at 5.5%. The annual interest expense triples from $20 million to $55 million. That $35 million difference must come from somewhere—either reduced investment, lower dividends, or higher prices passed to consumers. Multiply this across thousands of companies and you see the deflationary impact.

The composition of this maturity wall:

* US Treasuries: $2.2 trillion annually  
* European sovereigns: $1.8 trillion annually  
* Investment grade corporate: $1.2 trillion annually  
* High yield corporate: $400 billion annually  
* Emerging market sovereign and corporate: $800 billion annually  
* Real estate and structured finance: $600 billion annually

**CRITICAL INSIGHT: The Liquidity Squeeze Mechanism**

This maturity wall doesn't just represent refinancing—it creates a **crowding out effect**. As governments and corporations compete for scarce savings to refinance debt, they drive up interest rates and squeeze out marginal borrowers. This is particularly dangerous for:

6. **Highly leveraged companies** that cannot afford higher rates (zombie companies comprise \~15% of US public companies)  
7. **Commercial real estate** with $1.5T maturing 2025-2027 into a market where property values have declined 20-40%  
8. **Emerging markets** facing both higher rates and potential dollar strength  
9. **Private credit markets** where $600B in loans mature at floating rates 3-5% above current levels

# **7\. ASSET PRICE IMPLICATIONS**

The ultimate question for investors: ***How should I position my portfolio given current liquidity conditions?***

## **7.1 The Liquidity-Asset Price Transmission Mechanism**

Different asset classes respond to liquidity changes with different lag times:

* **Bitcoin/Crypto: 3-month lead** (most liquidity-sensitive assets react first)  
* **FX Markets: 3-month lead** (currency strength reflects relative liquidity)  
* **Bonds: 6-month lag** (inverse correlation: liquidity down \= bonds up)  
* **Equities: 6-9 month lag** (strongest historical correlation but delayed)  
* **Gold: Immediate** (acts as liquidity hedge and inflation hedge)

## **7.2 Specific Asset Class Recommendations**

**EQUITIES: Take profits, add hedges**  
US equities have benefited enormously from the liquidity expansion since Q4 2022\. The S\&P 500 has gained over 50% from the October 2022 bottom. However, with liquidity growth decelerating and the cycle approaching its peak, we recommend reducing equity beta from overweight to neutral. This doesn't mean selling everything—it means trimming winners, rotating from growth to value, and adding portfolio hedges (put options, VIX calls, inverse ETFs).

**GOLD: Core holding 10-15% allocation**  
Gold is the premier liquidity-crisis hedge. It rallies both when liquidity is expanding (as an inflation hedge) and when it's contracting (as a safe haven). Current levels around $2,700/oz reflect strong institutional demand. We project gold reaches $3,000-3,200/oz by Q2 2026 as the liquidity cycle peaks and geopolitical risk premiums rise. Gold miners offer leveraged exposure but come with operational risks.

**BITCOIN/CRYPTO: Reduce exposure at peaks**  
Bitcoin's 3-month lead time means current price action reflects liquidity conditions from Q3 2025\. The recent rally above $90K suggests liquidity peaked in late Q3. With the 3-month transmission lag, we expect Bitcoin to peak in Q4 2025 \- Q1 2026, followed by a significant correction (30-50%) as global liquidity contracts in H2 2026\. Use strength to reduce exposure.

**BONDS: Extend duration starting Q3 2026**  
The bond bear market that began in 2020 is approaching its end. As liquidity contracts in 2026-2027 and recession risks rise, bonds will rally. However, timing is critical—buying too early means fighting the Fed and suffering negative returns. We recommend beginning to extend duration (move from 2-year to 10-year Treasuries) in Q3 2026 when it becomes clear the liquidity peak has passed.
